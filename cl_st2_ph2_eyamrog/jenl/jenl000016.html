Reflections From Editors of Journal of English Linguistics

1. Introduction

Alexandra D’Arcy
University of Victoria
Peter J. Grund
Yale University
As Journal of English Linguistics (JEngL) reached fifty published volumes this year (2022) and as we as the current editors of the journal were contemplating the past and future of JEngL in light of this milestone, we were curious what past editors thought about their tenure at its helm. Rarely do we hear much about how editors think about their work or what their takeways from such work are. Devoted broadly to issues of the profession, our In the Profession column (introduced by the two former editors Anne Curzan and Robin Queen; see below) seemed an ideal place to explore their thoughts. We therefore invited previous editors to submit reflections on their experiences, prompted by questions such as “What do you take away from your tenure as JEngL editor?”; “How has the work influenced you professionally or impacted your scholarship?”; “What were the joys (and perhaps challenges) of being a JEngL editor?” Their thoughtful and wide-ranging answers follow below.
As current editors, we have refrained from adding our own specific reflections here. Being in the middle of the editorship and undertaking editorial work pretty much every day, we do not have the same (critical) distance as our predecessors. But our joys (and challenges) certainly mirror many of those singled out by previous editors. Editing involves a very substantial time commitment, and, like so many other aspects of academic life (and beyond!), journal editing has experienced significant challenges over the past couple of years. We suspect we are in the midst of a turning point in academic publishing as a result of the unsustainable pressure on especially early-career scholars to publish more and more journal articles and the concomitant stresses on the process of finding reviewers and obtaining timely reviews (see, e.g., the discussion in Warner 2022). But, despite the all-encompassing nature of journal editing, it is immensely rewarding to be able to shepherd exciting research through to completion, to support early-career scholars, and to work collaboratively to make the publication machinery run smoothly. And you cannot work collaboratively in this way without building meaningful connection with your co-editor, which is immensely rewarding as well. Even without the benefit of distance, as we think about this work, one thing stands out to us. We have been incredibly fortunate to have worked and to be working with a fantastic line-up of editorial assistants over the years: Christopher Strelluf, Lauren Fath, and Corinna Cook (for Peter and Matthew Gordon), and Candice Wuehle, Lucia Iglesias, Erika Grandstaff, and, at the time of writing, Sajib Ghosh (for Alex and Peter). As our submitting authors can attest (irrespective of whether their work is published or not), the professionalism, dedication, and hard work of our editorial assistants is remarkable, especially considering their many other commitments—their tenure with the journal has always coincided with their tenure as graduate students.
The future of JEngL is bright. We continue to publish thought-provoking and cutting-edge research, and with the support of our editorial board, we have been able to provide a venue for diverse voices in our field, whether from article authors, review writers, column writers, or interviewers and interviewees. We look forward to working with and learning from submitting authors and reviewers over the coming years, and to contributing to the continuing success of JEngL beyond this fifty-volume milestone.
William A. Kretzschmar, Jr.
University of Georgia and Uppsala University
Robert Peters, the founding editor of JEngL, produced his last issue in 1983 (Volume 16). He and his associate editor, Elizabeth Bowman, had produced camera-ready copy for all of his annual volumes (using the same typist the whole time!) at Western Washington University. In his announcement of the first change in editors, he thanked Raven McDavid as a frequent contributor to the journal. It was McDavid who suggested Peters’ replacement, me.
My first issue of the journal, Volume 17 (1984), made changes that fit the time and place. The issue was still published using camera-ready copy, only now produced using a desktop computer: “Camera-ready copy (80% reduction) for this issue of JEngL was prepared on a Morrow Micro Decision MD-3, using WordStar and FancyFont printer-driver software and an Epson Mx-100 printer” (copyright page note, JEngL, vol. 17, 1984). The IBM PC had come out in 1981, but the Morrow computer, using the CPM (Control Program for Microcomputers) operating system, was more affordable for the poor editor. WordStar was the dominant word processor of that time, and FancyFont was a program that allowed me to design fonts with phonetics and Old English characters that could be printed on a dot-matrix printer since the usual computers and printers did not offer those. Volume 17 (1984) featured articles by David Yerkes (“The Old Norse and Old English prose accounts of the Phoenix”) and David Shores (“The stressed vowels of the speech of Tangier Island, Virginia”) that used the new fonts extensively. My associate editor at that time was Michael Miller, like myself, one of McDavid’s PhDs. Four editorial assistants (Nancy De Rango, Alice MacNaughton, Dawn Orbison, and Linda Peterson) also received credit on the title page; they were undergraduate student interns who worked with me at the University of Wisconsin-Whitewater, as part of an arrangement with the university under which they got course credit, and editing JEngL counted as one course of my 4-4 teaching load.
JEngL expanded to two issues per volume the next year. I moved to the University of Georgia in 1986, and after that the expansion of issues of the journal and continuing computer development led to delays in publication. Issue 21(1) was the first issue to use the new typographical system I developed for a (then new) laser printer. As the note on the copyright page says, “Camera-ready copy (80% reduction) for this issue of JEngL was prepared using XyWrite III Plus word-processing software, FancyFont printer-driver software (SoftCraft Inc), and a Hewlett-Packard LaserJet Series II printer.” The laser printer made the type less “dotty” and more like what one would expect in a printed publication; indeed, we used it to make camera-ready copy for the LAMSAS handbook (Kretzschmar et al. 1993), since there were still no other good options for printing all the fine phonetics employed in the Linguistic Atlas. I am proud to have played a role in the development of computer methods for publishing in linguistics, but all the time involved in this aspect of the work slowed down publication of the journal. Issue 22(1), a special issue on “Computer methods in dialectology” (Kretzschmar et al. 1989) came out a year late in 1990, and Issue 22(2) came out even later in 1993. A combined Issue 23(1-2), a special issue in memory of Harold Allen, came out in 1995.
These delays led me to agree to let Sage Publications take over publication of the journal, beginning with Volume 24 in 1996, now as a quarterly. Michael Miller had died young of AIDS in the meantime, so Volume 24 was also Charles Meyer’s first as Associate Editor. When Sage started producing JEngL, they also agreed that I should be the editor of a book series on empirical linguistics. I lined up a number of authors and titles for the series, but in 1999 Sage decided not to continue the series after the commissioning editor, Alex Schwartz, left for another job. Because of that I decided to resign as editor of JEngL. My last volume included the completion of a promise: before he died, I had promised Michael that I would arrange to publish his monograph, Dynamics of a sociolinguistic system: English plural formation in Augusta, Georgia. I originally wanted to place it in my book series, but when that was no longer possible it appeared as Issue 27(3), which I edited with Ron Butters and Clai Rice (see Miller 1999).
I had a good run editing JEngL, including innovation along the way. I am particularly happy to have included a number of student interns in the work, and to have been able to work with so many people in our field as contributors to the journal.

Charles F. Meyer
University of Massachusetts, Boston
When I first became editor of the JEngL, in the Editor’s Note part of the first issue I edited, I promised to carry on the editorial processes that I had inherited from the editor preceding me, Bill Kretzschmar. Specifically, I would continue the high standards for publication that Bill had established, to make the editorial process as smooth as possible for both contributors to the journal and individuals who would be evaluating submissions for publication. I learned as I edited the journal to, as politely as possible, encourage reviewers to submit their reviews as quickly and promptly as possible. I also developed ways to break the news of a submission being rejected as politely and tactfully as possible. Of course, it was most rewarding to be able to contact an author and congratulate them on the acceptance of their submission.
Editing JEngL introduced me to all the complexities that are involved in the process of editing a scholarly journal. It became clear to me that the notion of time is very important in journal production. Issues have deadlines: they come out at specific times. However, for many academics (including myself), time is a flexible concept. For instance, for some reviewers, giving them the “deadline” of, say, a month to review a submission was a “request” rather than a “mandate.” So I learned to develop strategies to politely encourage reviewers to do their best to submit the reviews on time, and if they didn’t, I would “nudge” them to complete their review as quickly as possible. I cannot stress how important this is to journal production, especially to authors, who need to know as promptly as possible whether their submission was accepted or not. I must admit that there was great joy in telling someone that their submission had been accepted, and quite difficult to tell an author that their submission was rejected. But whether an article was accepted or rejected, I always made it a point to supply authors with not just my perspective on the submission but a synthesis of what the evaluators had said.
While editing a journal is a very complicated and time-consuming endeavor, I learned so much from my tenure with JEngL. For instance, it kept me abreast of all the differing types of empirical research that was being conducted, which benefited my own scholarship. Editing accepted submissions for publication greatly improved my own writing, as I was able to see how many different types of authors composed their research. I also learned how to mitigate the occasional reader report that was overly negative or critical so that the author, especially if they were junior faculty member, would not be devastated by the criticism. Fortunately, I never received too many “inappropriately negative” reviews. Reviewers were overwhelmingly positive and helpful in their comments, regardless of whether they were accepting or rejecting an article.
I left JEngL exhausted but very grateful for the opportunity to have edited a journal. It was a very rewarding experience to be able to read and evaluate all the interesting research that was being conducted by individuals who submitted their articles for consideration. It also made me appreciate all the hard work done by those who are editors of journals. Journals are the lifeblood of the academic world: they not only disseminate current information and ideas in the articles that scholars write but publishing in them is critical to academics who are in the process of getting tenure.
In closing, I would recommend to all academics that if given the opportunity, they should seriously consider doing some kind of editing, whether it’s a journal, a book with multiple contributions from different authors, or a book appearing in a book series. This kind of editing is a valuable service contribution to the profession as well as a way to reflect upon your own writing by examining closely how others write.
Anne Curzan
University of Michigan
Nine years went very quickly, and here’s the punchline (which I recognize is not funny, but it is true): I am a better scholar, writer, reviewer, mentor, and editor for those nine years co-editing JEngL.
One of the greatest joys of being an editor for JEngL was getting to participate in an extended, weekly, lively intellectual conversation about the field of English linguistics: with my co-editors (first Charles [Chuck] Meyer and then Robin Queen), with authors and reviewers, and with our graduate student editorial assistants. Being a journal editor kept me current with cutting-edge research as it was being completed, even when my life had lots of competing demands, because it was my job. And my co-editors and I had the privilege of supporting researchers in making their arguments as persuasive as possible and trying to expand the range of voices and work in “the field,” which journals help define. My time as an editor also underlined for me the incredible generosity of reviewers, who pored over articles and sent detailed comments for the authors and editors—for no compensation—and were regularly brilliant both in problem identification and problem solving. Now, of course, the reviewers didn’t always agree with each other in their brilliance, and I found it a rewarding intellectual puzzle to work with my co-editor to harmonize the reviews with our own feedback in order to provide coherent guidance to the authors. I learned as much as any author through the review process of each and every article.
It was also exciting to realize how journal editing can push the field through format. When Robin Queen stepped in as my co-editor, we created new features for the journal that we thought would be valuable contributions to the field: the annual interview with a major scholar in the field of English linguistics, and the column “In the Profession.” We wanted to put JEngL on the map in a distinctive way. Both these features, which were focused less on technical linguistic content and more on mentoring and humanizing the field, pushed the boundaries for publishing in linguistics journals. It is rewarding to see that both these innovations remain, years after we finished as editors. I would encourage anyone taking on an editor role to step back and think about how an academic journal can contribute to the field, beyond publishing outstanding research articles aimed primarily at specialized audiences.
I would be remiss not to add one more very clear memory: for those nine years, I always had one or more hard copies of articles in my bag that needed copy-editing—work I did everywhere: waiting for an appointment, sitting on an airplane, passing time while the car was undergoing maintenance, you name it. Now I carry a novel. Or perhaps the latest issue of JEngL so that I can benefit from the thoughtful work the current editors are doing.
Robin Queen
University of Michigan
I remember sitting with Anne Curzan at the 2005 Annual Meeting of the Linguistic Society of America and thinking about what innovations we might want to introduce as I joined her to co-edit JEngL. It was very much a “sky’s the limit” moment. As we batted around ideas and ideals, we homed in on the “In the Profession” column and the year-end interview with a prominent figure in English linguistics broadly conceived. Both of those ideas took flight and still fly around in the journal, which is gratifying to see now over a decade later.
I think often about my time at JEngL as I settle into the third third of my career as a linguist. I learned to see that anything (though not everything) is possible with the right resources and capacities in place and that many ideas are short-lived while others take hold and become part of “how it’s always been.” Co-editing JEngL taught me my first lessons in collaboration, having not been trained to engage in collaborative scholarship. It’s a different kind of collaboration than a research collaboration, but in many ways, it better prepared me for other kinds of collaborations that have come my way in the intervening years, particularly as connected to administration.
Editing JEngL helped me understand the joy of the process, soup to nuts. I knew the first part of writing, sending off, and revising a manuscript based on Reviewer #1’s, Reviewer #2’s (oh, that Reviewer #2), and the general editor’s comments. When I became a general editor, I also learned about soliciting manuscripts and moving them through to publication. Both ends of the process can bring joy, but they are different kinds of joy. I liked seeing articles move through the publication queue, interacting with authors, giving advice about how to interpret the anonymous reviews, and finally hitting “accept” on the manuscript. I just took a glance at the “top cited” page for the journal and was proud to see that the top five articles on the list were published during my co-tenure. And, that I remember each one.
My personal research benefitted, too, as I became newly aware of trends in scholarship that helped inform my own work. Seeing those trends (and the range of possibilities within them) helped convince me to write my book, Vox popular (Queen 2015). Co-editing the journal further gave me the confidence to engage in a range of higher-level activities: co-running a Linguistic Society Summer Institute, becoming a chair, remaining a chair, and taking on ad hoc chances to really make a difference about things I care about.
If I were giving advice to a potential editor about whether to edit JEngL, I guess I’d say, do it if you can do it with your whole heart and dreams and hopes. It’s a way of helping shape the field that endures.

Matthew J. Gordon
University of Missouri
I reflect on my tenure as co-editor of JEngL with a mix of fondness for the engagement with cutting-edge research and wonder at how I ever found the time to do all that work. When I started my editorship in 2012, the journal had had Anne Curzan and Robin Queen at the helm for many years. They had continued to raise the journal’s profile, especially in fields like sociolinguistics and corpus linguistics, and had introduced features that distinguished JEngL from other venues. The annual interview with an influential scholar was one such new addition, and I continue to look forward to those contributions every December.
JEngL presents a challenge to any editor because it covers research in such a wide range of subfields. I had the good fortune of working with Peter Grund as co-editor. Our areas of expertise complement each other, and this proved helpful in evaluating many of the manuscripts submitted to the journal. To anyone contemplating the editorship of a journal with the volume and range of submissions of JEngL, I strongly recommend collaborating with one or more co-editors. The time involved in reading manuscripts, finding appropriate reviewers, crafting decision letters, offering feedback to authors, copy-editing accepted manuscripts, and the many other editorial tasks is substantial, and I certainly valued the opportunity to split that workload with Peter. I found that the team approach also makes for a better product as authors benefit from suggestions that help them speak to a wider audience.
What I value most about my experience at JEngL is how it expanded my perspective on the vast wealth of scholarship in English linguistics. Before 2012, for example, I had limited familiarity with corpus linguistics, and even though I had taught a course in the history of English for years, my knowledge of Old and Middle English did not go much beyond a textbook-level overview. The many manuscripts in these fields that we received brought me in touch with new techniques of analysis and key theoretical debates, serving as the equivalent of a multi-year graduate seminar. My intellectual horizon was also expanded geographically due to the many contributions to JEngL from scholars outside North America. As someone who finds most research carried out in mainstream American (Chomskyan) grammatical theory to be impenetrable, I was overjoyed to read submissions to JEngL from European scholars and others containing insightful analyses of English syntax and morphology that I could actually understand and appreciate.
I am grateful for the personal intellectual growth my editing experience afforded as well as for the satisfaction that came from shepherding so many innovative papers through the process of publication. JEngL has always been a venue that features research from authors with a range of experience, and I am especially gratified by our efforts to help junior scholars bring their work to the journal’s readership. I am proud to have played a small part in the history of this influential journal.
