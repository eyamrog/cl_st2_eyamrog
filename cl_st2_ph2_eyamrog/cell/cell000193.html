    Summary

    During respiration, humans breathe in more than 10,000 liters of non-sterile air daily, allowing some pathogens access to alveoli. Interestingly, alveoli outnumber alveolar macrophages (AMs), which favors alveoli devoid of AMs. If AMs, like most tissue macrophages, are sessile, then this numerical advantage would be exploited by pathogens unless neutrophils from the blood stream intervened. However, this would translate to omnipresent persistent inflammation. Developing in vivo real-time intravital imaging of alveoli revealed AMs crawling in and between alveoli using the pores of Kohn. Importantly, these macrophages sensed, chemotaxed, and, with high efficiency, phagocytosed inhaled bacterial pathogens such as P. aeruginosa and S. aureus, cloaking the bacteria from neutrophils. Impairing AM chemotaxis toward bacteria induced superfluous neutrophil recruitment, leading to inappropriate inflammation and injury. In a disease context, influenza A virus infection impaired AM crawling via the type II interferon signaling pathway, and this greatly increased secondary bacterial co-infection.

    Patrolling AMs Detect and Actively Crawl toward Inhaled Bacteria

    We next wanted to find out how AMs respond to the presence of pathogens. To this end, we administered several doses (103–106) of GFP expressing Pseudomonas aeruginosa (P. aeruginosa) directly into the lungs of anesthetized mice. Using FACS, P. aeruginosa could be detected in AMs (P. aeruginosa+ AMs) at doses between 104–106, albeit at very low numbers at the lower concentration and not at all at 103 (Figure 3A). Thus, we decided to use the dose of 106 CFUs for our experiments. At 30 min (data not shown) and even 2 h post infection (p.i.) (Figure 3B), AMs were the predominant immune cells that had captured inhaled P. aeruginosa, with only very few non-AM immune cells, including neutrophils, harboring bacteria at this concentration (106 colony-forming units [CFUs]) (Figures 3A and 3B). To confirm that P. aeruginosa was in AMs, a transparency map was created (Figure 3C), and as the transparency of PKH-AMs was increased (less green), P. aeruginosa came into view (red particles), consistent with bacteria being intracellular in AMs. Notably, when AMs were depleted with chlodronate liposomes (Figure 3D) and then P. aeruginosa was administered, a significant increase in the amount of total neutrophils (Figure 3E) and P. aeruginosa+ neutrophils in the BALF was noted (Figure 3F). Moreover, in the absence of AMs, higher CFUs of “free” P. aeruginosa were also found in alveoli (Figure 3G). These observations highlight the importance of AMs as an extremely effective pathogen uptake mechanism that conceals inhaled pathogens from the rest of the immune system.

    AMs utilize C5a and to a lesser extent formylated peptides as chemoattractants

    We assessed the transcripts of chemokine receptors in AMs and noted that AMs expressed very high levels of C5a receptor/CD88 (C5ar1) and formylated peptide receptors (Fpr1 and Fpr2) (Figure 4E; Figure S3I). We first tested whether complement components were important in capture of inhaled bacteria by utilizing C3 KO mice (C3−/− mice lacking C3). Two hours p.i. with P. aeruginosa into the lungs of WT or C3 KO mice, we recovered 5-fold higher CFUs of P. aeruginosa in the BALF of C3 KO mice (Figure 4F). This suggested that there were many more free bacteria in the alveolar spaces of C3 KO compared with WT mice, perhaps because of a lack of chemotaxis. Interestingly, no differences in the amount P. aeruginosa+ AMs were observed (Figure 4G), suggesting more growth of bacteria in the mutant mice. To further explore this, we removed the ability of bacteria to replicate by fixing P. aeruginosa and administered them directly into the lungs of WT or C3 KO mice. This resulted in a significant reduction in P. aeruginosa+ AMs in C3 KO mice (Figure 4H). Moreover, using FACS, we were able to find higher numbers of free fixed GFP+ P. aeruginosa in the BALF of C3 KO compared with WT mice, supporting the concept that complement fragments play a role in chemotaxis of AMs toward bacteria (Figure 4I). Next we tested whether these results were due to C5a-C5ar1-mediated chemotaxis. We administered a C5ar1-blocking antibody directly into the lungs of naive mice, followed by administration of live or fixed P. aeruginosa. Higher numbers of fixed P. aeruginosa or live bacteria were found in the BALF of anti-C5ar1-treated compared with isotype-treated mice (Figure 4J; Figure S3J). However, P. aeruginosa+ AMs were reduced only when fixed P. aeruginosa was administered (Figure 4K; Figure S3K).
    We also tested whether bacterial metabolites, such as formylated peptides, attract AMs by utilizing FPR-1 KO mice (Fpr1−/− mice lacking FPR-1). Interestingly, lack of only FPR-1 did not impair the ability of AMs to capture inhaled P. aeruginosa (Figures S3L and S3M). We set up an in vitro chemotaxis assay to validate these results. In in vitro chemotaxis assays, AM migration toward P. aeruginosa was partially impaired when C5ar1 was blocked and was intact with FPR-1 KO AMs (Figure 4L). However, when C5ar1 was additionally blocked in FPR-1 KO AMs, AM chemotaxis was further reduced to 25% of control levels (Figure 4L). Collectively, these results suggest that AMs utilize rapidly generated C5a and, to a lesser extent, bacterial metabolites, including formylated peptides. However, these experiments also highlight the tremendous redundancy in the system, so for further functional experiments, PTx was used to globally impair AM chemotaxis.

    AM Ability to Conceal Inhaled Bacteria Reduces Overt Lung Inflammation

    We noted that inhalation of 106 CFUs of P. aeruginosa crossed a threshold for neutrophil recruitment into the BALF (Figure S3A). Indeed, at 2 h p.i. with P. aeruginosa, 1,000–10,000 neutrophils were present in the BALF, which was not affected by blocking AM chemotaxis ability at this early time point (Figure 5A, first panel). It is worth noting that BALF neutrophils 2 h after P. aeruginosa made up only about 0.5% of the total neutrophils that could be found in whole-lung homogenates (Figure S3N). This suggested that most of the lung neutrophils were in the pulmonary vasculature, adherent to the endothelium. Interestingly, almost 20% of the neutrophils in BALF were P. aeruginosa+ when AM chemotaxis was impaired, whereas less than 1% of neutrophils were P. aeruginosa+ when the AMs were fully functional (Figure 5B, first panel). This is consistent with functional AMs scavenging many more P. aeruginosa, leaving very few available for neutrophils. Over the next 4 h, twice as many neutrophils were recruited into the BALF of PTx pretreated than control mice (Figure 5A, second panel), suggesting that free bacteria signal more neutrophils to infiltrate into alveoli, and higher numbers of neutrophils were P. aeruginosa+ in BALF of PTx pretreated mice at 6 h p.i. (Figure 5B, second panel).
    Next we performed a chemokine/cytokine array of the BALF supernatant and noted that impairing AM chemotaxis significantly enhanced production of the neutrophil-specific chemokines MIP-2 and KC (Figure 5C). Moreover, greater production of pro-inflammatory cytokines, such as interleukin-6 (IL-6), as well as cytokines that mobilize neutrophils from bone marrow, such as G-CSF and GM-CSF, were observed when AM chemotaxis was impaired (Figure 5C). We also noted increased leakage of proteins into the BALF (Figure 5D). FACS and histological examinations indicated heightened neutrophil infiltration and inflammation (Figure 5E; Figure S3O). By 24 h and 5 days, P. aeruginosa was detected in more mice when AMs were pretreated with PTx, despite higher neutrophil numbers in the lungs (Figures 5E and 5F). These observations were similar to the results seen when AMs were depleted prior to infection (Figures 3E–3G). Taken together, these results suggest that, when AMs are not able to contain the inhaled P. aeruginosa, neutrophil responses are enhanced, which can lead to deleterious inflammation and injury of the lung and less efficient clearance of P. aeruginosa.

    In vivo labeling of AMs

Ultrathin sections were cut with diamond knife on an ultramicrotome (Ultracut E; Reichert-Jung) and collected on single-hole grids with Formvar supporting film. The sections were stained with aqueous uranyl acetate and Reynold’s lead citrate and observed under Hitachi H7650TEM at 80 kV. The images were acquired with AMT16000 digital camera mounted on the microscope.

    Histology and analysis

    Mice were euthanized with CO2. The trachea was exposed and cannulated with 20G catheter. The lungs were inflated with 10% neutral buffered formalin (fixative), excised, and immersed in fixative for 24 hours and subsequently embedded in paraffin. Sections (4 μm) were prepared and stained with hematoxylin and eosin. Slides were reviewed by a blinded pulmonary clinical pathologist.
