    Summary

    Cognitive dysfunction and reactive microglia are hallmarks of traumatic brain injury (TBI), yet whether these cells contribute to cognitive deficits and secondary inflammatory pathology remains poorly understood. Here, we show that removal of microglia from the mouse brain has little effect on the outcome of TBI, but inducing the turnover of these cells through either pharmacologic or genetic approaches can yield a neuroprotective microglial phenotype that profoundly aids recovery. The beneficial effects of these repopulating microglia are critically dependent on interleukin-6 (IL-6) trans-signaling via the soluble IL-6 receptor (IL-6R) and robustly support adult neurogenesis, specifically by augmenting the survival of newborn neurons that directly support cognitive function. We conclude that microglia in the mammalian brain can be manipulated to adopt a neuroprotective and pro-regenerative phenotype that can aid repair and alleviate the cognitive deficits arising from brain injury.

    Active place avoidance

    To assess hippocampal-dependent spatial learning and memory, mice were tested in the active place avoidance (APA) task, as previously described (Willis et al., 2017

Briefly, the APA testing first involved daily handling of the mice after surgery prior to behavioral testing, followed by habituation of the mice to the rotating (clockwise, 1rpm) APA testing arena for 20min, during which time the mice could freely explore with the shock zone turned off. The APA testing arena consisted of an elevated arena with a grid metal floor, fenced by a 32cm-high clear Perspex circular boundary, with black and white A3 size visual cues placed on the 4 surrounding walls. One day after habituation, mice were tested in the APA task, during which they were tasked to avoid a 60° shock zone, the position of which did not move in relation to the room coordinates. In-session acquisition of spatial learning was assessed by the number of entries into the shock zone during each 5 min interval of the 20min trial. Longer-term spatial learning and memory was assessed by the total number of entries or shock during each day, across 5 days of testing. The number of entries into the shock zone, the number of shocks received, and the distance traveled during the trial were analyzed using Track Analysis software (Bio-Signal Group).

    Morris water maze

    Mice underwent testing in the Morris water maze starting at 12 days post-injury. The apparatus consisted of a circular swimming pool (100 cm in diameter, 20 cm high) filled with water (21–25°C), which was opaque due to the addition of non-toxic white paint. Mice were required to locate a submerged platform using the spatial cues located around the arena. For the first 5 days of testing, the platform was located within the north-east quadrant of the testing arena, hidden beneath 1.5 cm of opaque water. Each day, over four trials, mice were allowed 60 s to locate the hidden platform, with an inter-trial interval of 15 min. At the start of each trial the mouse was released from one of four starting positions (south, west, north or east), chosen in a pseudo-random order. If the mouse was unable to locate the platform within 60 s it was guided there, and allowed to remain on the platform for 10 s. The average latency to locating the platform over the four trials for each test day was recorded using Ethovision software (Noldus Information Technology, version 11.5.1022). On days 7 and 8, a reversal learning procedure was performed, with the platform being re-introduced into the testing arena in the south-west quadrant. During reversal learning, mice were again trained to find the platform over four 60 s trials, with 15-min inter-trial intervals.

    Y-maze

    As a further test of spatial learning and memory following traumatic brain injury (TBI), mice were also tested in the Y-maze task 30 days post-injury. The Y-maze apparatus was composed of two arms connected to a runway (home arm), each 45 × 15 × 15 cm. The arms contained different visual cues, consisting of black and white symbols/shapes on laminated A4 size paper. The maze was positioned 1 m above the ground, and the mice were tracked by an overhead camera. During training, the animals were placed in the home arm with access to only one arm for 3 min. This training arm was alternated for each mouse. Following a 20-min intermission, mice were placed back into the Y-maze for 3 min with access to the training and novel arms. The time spent in the novel and the training arms, as well as the distance traveled, were analyzed using Ethovision software (Noldus Information Technology, version 11.5.1022).

    Spatial transcriptomics

    Brain samples (n = 3 per condition) were snap-frozen using the isopentane and dry ice procedure, before being embedded into Optimal Cutting Temperature (OCT) blocks. Samples were cut using Leica Cryostat and 10μm thick sections were collected onto library preparation (LP) glass slides, that were then fixed with 4% formaldehyde solution for 10 min at room temperature. Sections were stained with Mayer’s hematoxylin for 10 min, followed by 1-minute incubation in Bluing Buffer and counterstaining with Eosin for 3 min at room temperature. H&E images were captured using Zeiss Axio Imager at 20x Magnification. ST sequencing libraries were prepared as per manufacturer’s instructions, with pre-permeabalisation and permeabalisation performed with 20 and 7 min incubations in collagenase and trypsin, respectively. On-slide cDNA synthesis, tissue removal, probe cleavage, and library preparation were performed as per the manual. High-quality sequencing libraries were obtained from 107-698 ng and with the average length at 660-780 bp.
    ST libraries were sequenced on the Illumina Nextseq500, using a 150 cycles kit with read configuration as read 1 (26 bp) and read 2 (124 bp). Raw sequencing data in BCL format were converted to FASTQ reads using bcl2fastq/2.17. Reads were mapped to the mouse reference genome GRCm38 using STAR-2.7.1a and gene annotation GRCm38.96.gtf. We obtained 46-72 million high-quality, uniquely mapped reads for the six libraries. On average, each ST spot had 528-3345 genes detected, with maximum genes per spot ranging from 3482-9526 genes. Gene-count matrices were generated using an ST pipeline; ST spot coordinates were adjusted using st_spot_detector with inputs from the H&E images and Cy3 images. The dentate gyrus and hippocampus regions were identified on H&E images. Gene expression per spot in the selected regions was plotted on tissue images using Python scripts available in our stlearn github repository.

    Quantification and Statistical Analysis

    Investigator blinding to experimental conditions was applied and all statistical analyses were conducted using GraphPad Prism software (version 7.02). Statistical differences were determined using either an unpaired two-tailed Student’s t test, one-way ANOVA, two-way ANOVA or repeated-measures two-way ANOVA, as appropriate. For all ANOVAs, a Greenhouse-Geisser correction and Bonferroni post hoc comparison were used. The value of n reported within figure legends represents number of animals, with appropriateness of sample sizes for key results confirmed by power analysis, where power = 0.8 and p < 0.05. There is no missing data nor were any animals removed post hoc. Values are represented as mean ± SEM, with differences considered significant when p < 0.05.
