    Summary

    Nucleotide-binding, leucine-rich repeat receptors (NLRs) are major immune receptors in plants and animals. Upon activation, the Arabidopsis NLR protein ZAR1 forms a pentameric resistosome in vitro and triggers immune responses and cell death in plants. In this study, we employed single-molecule imaging to show that the activated ZAR1 protein can form pentameric complexes in the plasma membrane. The ZAR1 resistosome displayed ion channel activity in Xenopus oocytes in a manner dependent on a conserved acidic residue Glu11 situated in the channel pore. Pre-assembled ZAR1 resistosome was readily incorporated into planar lipid-bilayers and displayed calcium-permeable cation-selective channel activity. Furthermore, we show that activation of ZAR1 in the plant cell led to Glu11-dependent Ca2+ influx, perturbation of subcellular structures, production of reactive oxygen species, and cell death. The results thus support that the ZAR1 resistosome acts as a calcium-permeable cation channel to trigger immunity and cell death.

    The ZAR1 complex possesses channel activity in Xenopus oocytes in a manner dependent on Glu11

    One of the most remarkable features of the ZAR1 resistosome is the central pore through the pentameric axis of the assembled complex (Wang et al., 2019a
    66.
    Wang, J. ∙ Hu, M. ∙ Wang, J. ...
    Reconstitution and structure of a plant NLR resistosome conferring immunity

The pore is divided into a central cavity and an intracellular cavity, with a pronounced constriction (∼1.7 Å in radius) near the carboxylate ring of Glu134 in the middle of the ion permeation path (Figure 1B). Within the central cavity, other acidic residues, including Glu11, Glu18, Glu19, Asp26, Asp33, Glu37, Glu120, and Glu130, form additional carboxylate rings that line the pore and render the inner surface generally electro-negative (Figure 1C). This structural feature likely contributes to conducting cations across membranes. While the channel pore hypothesis agrees with a body of previous observations, direct evidence for it is still lacking.

Expression of ZAR1 along with RKS1, PBL2, and AvrAC led to cell death in protoplasts 8–9 h after transfection (Figure S1A). Our previous study showed that simultaneous substitutions of Glu11 and Glu18 to Ala abolished immunity and cell death but not the oligomerization activity (Wang et al., 2019a

    The ZAR1 resistosome possesses cation-selective channel activity in planar lipid-bilayers

    To better characterize the channel activity of the ZAR1 resistosome, we reconstituted the pre-assembled ZAR1 resistosome complex (pentameric ZAR1-RKS1-PBL2UMP) into the planar lipid bilayers and conducted single-channel measurements in symmetrical or asymmetrical solutions (Figure 2A). In the symmetrical solutions of 150 mM NaCl in both trans and cis chambers, the application of a series of transmembrane potentials to the trans side resulted in square step-like currents with a linear single-channel I-V relationship (Figures 2B and 2E), confirming that the ZAR1 resistosome forms an ion-conducting pore in the bilayer. To further examine the permeability property of the ZAR1 resistosome, single-channel recordings were carried out in asymmetrical solution, replacing 150 mM NaCl with 150 mM Na-Gluconate in the trans side. We observed similar apparent unitary currents with a linear single-channel I-V relationship (Figures 2C and 2E), suggesting that Na+ is the permeant ion under these conditions, as gluconate is an impermeable anion, whose dimension is much larger than 3.4 Å at the narrowest constriction of the ZAR1 resistosome pore. To rule out the possibility of conducting Cl− by the ZAR1 resistosome, we replaced the trans solution with 150 mM tetraethylammonium chloride (TEA-Cl), and the recorded currents displayed a rectification characteristic, with only negative currents observed under applied voltages ranging from −60 mV to +60 mV (Figures 2D and 2E). The rectifying currents suggest that the ZAR1 resistosome is impermeable to chloride anions as no positive current was observed under positive voltages applied. Considering that the cis chamber (150 mM NaCl) is grounded, the negative current is composed of Na+ ion flux from cis to trans side, as TEA is a large cation with a radius of 3.85 Å and is not permeable to known ion channels. Altogether, these observations demonstrate that the ZAR1 resistosome forms a channel-like pore, with selectivity for cation ions.

    Limitations of the study

    We show that single-molecule imaging is a powerful approach to studying the assembly of resistosomes in the plant cell. It should be noted, however, that the analysis relied on transient overexpression of the host components and pathogen effector. The future challenge will be to determine whether the native ZAR1 protein displays the same dynamics during pathogen infection. Another limitation of the study is with the cell death analysis. While it is clear that the ZAR1 channel activity and calcium flux are required for cell death triggering, the direct executor of cell death remains elusive and will require future studies.
