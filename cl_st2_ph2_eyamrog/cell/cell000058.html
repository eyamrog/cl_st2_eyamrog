    Summary

    It has generally proven challenging to produce functional β cells in vitro. Here, we describe a previously unidentified protein C receptor positive (Procr+) cell population in adult mouse pancreas through single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq). The cells reside in islets, do not express differentiation markers, and feature epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition characteristics. By genetic lineage tracing, Procr+ islet cells undergo clonal expansion and generate all four endocrine cell types during adult homeostasis. Sorted Procr+ cells, representing ∼1% of islet cells, can robustly form islet-like organoids when cultured at clonal density. Exponential expansion can be maintained over long periods by serial passaging, while differentiation can be induced at any time point in culture. β cells dominate in differentiated islet organoids, while α, δ, and PP cells occur at lower frequencies. The organoids are glucose-responsive and insulin-secreting. Upon transplantation in diabetic mice, these organoids reverse disease. These findings demonstrate that the adult mouse pancreatic islet contains a population of Procr+ endocrine progenitors.

    Validate and Visualize Procr+ Cells in Islets

    To characterize this novel Procr+ population, we micro-dissected islets and non-islet tissues. By negative selection for blood lineage cells and positive selection for epithelial cells, we separated islet cells into Procr+ (Lin−, EpCAM+, Procr+) and Procr− (Lin−, EpCAM+, Procr−) populations by cell sorting (Figure 2A) and performed qPCR analysis. Considering that rare mesenchymal, stellate, and endothelial cells (ECs) can express Procr as shown by scRNA-seq (Figure 1C), Procr+ and Procr− cells were isolated from the non-islet tissue as well. qPCR analysis indicated that signature genes of the new population (e.g., Rspo1, Fgf1, Upk3b, Hoxa5, Nkain4, Lgals7, Igfbp5, and Igf1) are exclusively expressed in Procr+ islet cells (Figure 2B).

    Islet-like Organoids Can Be Expanded In Vitro for Long-Term

    Next, we sought to expand these organoids through passaging. Dissociated single cells were replated every 7–8 days at a ratio of 1:4–1:6 in the same culture condition, supplemented with fresh ECs (see STAR Methods). In this culture system, the average organoid size remained stable during passaging (Figures 6A and 6B). However, the number of organoids expanded by 3- to 7-fold with each passage (Figure 6C). Organoids could be passaged at least 20 times, or 6 months, with no deceleration of the expansion rate (Figure 6C).
    When these passaged organoids were taken out of the expansion cycle and allowed to mature for an additional 3 weeks (i.e., 28–30 days upon being replated as single cells) (Figure 6D), mature organoids resembled the primary organoids described above. 7th and 15th passage organoids are given as examples, showing similar percentages of α, β, δ, and PP cells (Figures 6E, 6F, S5A, and S5B), glucose sensing, insulin and C-peptide secretion abilities, insulin granule numbers, and Ca2+ response when compared to the primary passage organoids (Figures 6G–6I and S5C–S5I). Organoid cultures maintained normal chromosome numbers over time (Figures S5J and S5K). These results implied that the expansion and differentiation capacities of the cells were maintained for long-term.

    Long-Term Cultured Organoids Reverse Diabetes In Vivo

    To test the full potential of the long-term cultured organoids, they were engrafted into the kidney capsule of streptozotocin (STZ)-induced diabetic mice, and the mice were monitored for glycemic improvement. 1,000 organoids (from the 7th or 15th passage, taken at day 7 of culture) or 300 freshly isolated islets, containing similar cell numbers (∼300,000 cells), were transplanted per recipient mouse. At 1 week post-transplantation, blood glucose levels were clearly reduced (Figure 7A), and body weight loss was stopped (Figure S6A). The glycemic improvement was maintained throughout the analysis period (Figure 7A). Notably, the organoid-transplanted groups exhibited similar rescue effects as the fresh islet-transplanted group (Figure 7A). At 1 month post-transplantation, mice transplanted with organoids exhibited glucose clearance ability and serum insulin levels almost identical to the mice receiving fresh islets (Figures 7B and 7C). Similar ameliorating effects were observed at 4 months post-transplantation (Figure S6B). Removal of the transplants by nephrectomy resulted in abrupt increase of blood glucose, confirming that the functional β cells reside in the kidney transplants (Figure 7A). Thus, the transplanted organoids were capable of secreting insulin and ameliorating progressive hyperglycemia in a diabetic mouse model.
    The grafted organoid cells were excised and analyzed after 1 month and 4 months of transplantation. All four endocrine cell types were detected, with the vast majority being Ins+ β cells (Figures 7D, 7E, and S6C), similar to the grafted islet cells (Figures S6D and S6E). The host pancreases were also analyzed at 1 month and 4 months post-transplantation and no sign of recovery of β cell numbers was noted (Figure S6F). These observations further supported that the ameliorating effects were due to the grafted cells in the kidney. The identities of these cells were further analyzed by scRNA-seq. The grafted cells, after maturation in vivo, clustered with primary endocrine cells (Figures 7F and S7A–S7C). In particular, the genes that encode islet cell markers, hormones, and transcriptional regulators were expressed at comparable levels between the primary mouse endocrine cells and the grafted organoid cells (Figures 7G, S7D, and S7E).

    Discussion

    In the current study, we identified a population of endocrine progenitor cells in adult mouse pancreas. These cells do not express known mature endocrine cell markers, feature EMT characteristic, and express Procr on their surface. In vivo, Procr+ islet cells undergo clonal expansion and give rise to all four endocrine cell types during adult homeostasis. In vitro, isolated Procr+ islet cells can grow at clonal density into islet-like organoids, which comprise all endocrine cell types and are capable of glucose-sensing and insulin secretion. These organoids can be exponentially expanded in vitro by serial passage over long time periods. These cultured islet-like organoids can reverse diabetes in vivo.

    Method Details

    Islets enrichment and purification

    Mouse pancreas was perfused by injection of 2-4 mL digestion buffer (RPMI 1640 medium containing type IV collagenase (1000-1500 U ml-1), 10% FBS (fetal bovine serum) and 25 mM HEPES) from the common duct. The pancreas was then dissected carefully to keep the integrity of the perfused tissue and then incubated at 37°C for 5-6 min followed by gently shaking for 3-6 min. The proper perfusion and digestion time are critical for the islets yield and structure integrity. After digestion, the tissue was suspended in 20 mL HBSS with 10% FBS and rigorously shook up and down for 40 times within 20 s, followed by HBSS wash for 2 times. The digested tissue was then filtered with 500 μm cell strainers. To enrich islets, the filtered sample underwent density gradient centrifugation or was transferred under the microscope for hand-picking of islets. To prepare the density gradient centrifugation, the tissue was first re-suspended in 10 mL histopaque-1077 and slowly covered by 10 mL HBSS without breaking the interface. Then centrifuge at 2,400 rpm for 22 min with the slowest acceleration and breaking to obtain the enriched islets compartment at the HBSS/histopaque-1077 interface. After the initial density gradient centrifugation step, the purity of islets ranged from 20% to 70%, depending on the quality of earlier perfusion and digestion process. To enhance the purity of islets, the samples were re-suspended in HBSS with 10% FBS for hand-pick. Islets with high purity were obtained by hand-pick under dissecting microscope for at least 3 rounds.

    Islets X-gal staining

    X-gal staining of purified islets was performed following the standard protocol with minor changes. In brief, islets were fixed in Fix A solution (1% Formaldehyde, 0.2% Glutaraldehyde, 0.02% NP-40 in PBS) at 4°C for 30 min, washed with PBS for 3 times, then stained in X-gal staining solution (1 mg ml-1 X-gal, 5 mM K3[Fe(CN)6], 5 mM K4[Fe(CN)6], 2 mM MgCl2 in PBS) at room temperature for 5 hr. After staining, islets were washed with PBS and the whole mount images were captured by Zeiss inverted microscope.

    Immunofluorescence, whole mount staining and microscopy

    To prepare samples for frozen sections, the dissected pancreas was fixed in 4% PFA at 4°C for 4 hr, washed with PBS for 3 times and then embedded with Optimum Cutting Temperature (OCT). Tissue sections were incubated with primary antibodies at 4°C overnight, followed by washing in PBST (PBS+0.1% Triton X-100), incubation with secondary antibodies and DAPI for 2 hr at room temperature, then sections were washed and mounted for confocal imaging.
    To perform whole mount staining, islets or cultured organoids were fixed in 4% PFA at 4°C for 1 hr, followed by 3 times of PBS washing, then blocked with the whole mount blocking buffer at room temperature for 1hr. The blocking buffer contained 10% FBS in PBST. Primary antibodies were diluted in blocking buffer and incubated at 4°C overnight, after washing with PBST for 3 times, the secondary antibodies and DAPI were incubated at 4°C overnight. Islets or organoids were then washed and mounted with mounting medium for confocal imaging.
    All confocal images were captured by Leica SP8 confocal detection system fitted on a Leica DMI6000 microscope.

    Preparation of islets single cell suspension and FACS analysis

    Purified islet samples were treated with 2 mL 0.05% trypsin-EDTA at 37°C for 4 min, followed by digestion with 0.1 mg ml-1 DNase I for 4 min, with gently pipetting for about 20 times. The digested cells were filtered through 70 μm cell strainer.
    For FACS analysis and cell sorting, antibody incubation was performed on ice for 25 min in HBSS with 5% FBS at a dilution of 1:200. All analysis and sorting experiments were performed using FACS Jazz (Becton Dickinson). The purity of the sorted population was routinely checked and ensured to be more than 95%.

    Co-culture and passage of mouse pancreatic islet organoids

    Cd31+ endothelial cells (ECs) were FACS-isolated from inguinal fat pad or skin of wild-type or Actin-dsRed mice. In detail, minced fat pad or skin were digested in digestion buffer (RPMI 1640 medium containing type III collagenase (300 U ml-1), 10% FBS (fetal bovine serum) and 25 mM HEPES) for 1 hr. After lysis of the red blood cells, a single-cell suspension was obtained by sequential incubation with 0.05% trypsin-EDTA at 37°C for 5 min and 0.1 mg ml-1 DNase I (Sigma) for 5 min with gentle pipetting, followed by filtration through 70 μm cell strainers. Subsequently, endothelial cells were collected by FACS sorting using Cd31 antibody. The mouse islet EpCAM+, Procr+/− cells were FACS-sorted from hand-picked islets of wild-type ICR, C57BL/6 or FVB mice as described above.
    Before plating, each well of 48-well was coated with 200 μL Matrigel, the plate was transferred into 37°C incubator for Matrigel gelification. The sorted islet cells and ECs were centrifuged and re-suspended in mouse organoid culture medium (DMEM/F12 plus 1% Penicillin-Streptomycin, 2% B27, 1% ITS, 50 ng ml-1 EGF, 2 μg ml-1 heparin, 10 ng ml-1 FGF2 and 5 ng ml-1 VEGFa). Islet cells and ECs were mixed in a cell number ratio of 1:3, and plated into the coated 48-wells.
    The medium was refreshed every 2-3 days. In order to induce β cell maturation, glucose challenges were performed. 20 mM glucose in HBSS was mixed with the organoid medium in a ratio of 1:1 to prepare high glucose medium, in which the final glucose concentration is 12.5 mM. Organoids were first cultured for 12 hr in low glucose (5 mM) medium followed by 12 hr treatment in high glucose (12.5 mM) medium for 3 rounds in total.
    Organoids were passaged every 7-8 days, occasionally as late as every 14 days. For passage, organoids were released from the Matrigel using dispase (37°C for 40 min) and dissociated into single cells with 1 mL 0.05% trypsin-EDTA (37°C for 6-9 min). Due to the expansion of islet cells in culture, the percentage of Cd31+ ECs at the end of primary passage was about 2%–6% of total cells (by FACS quantification), which was not sufficient to support the organoid expansion in the subsequent passage. Therefore, during each passage, the digested single cell suspension was replenished with freshly isolated Cd31+ ECs in a cell number ratio of 1:1, and then the mixture was re-plated in a split ratio of 1:4-1:6.

    Glucose-stimulated insulin and C-peptide secretion

    About 100 mouse organoids (at day 28 culture) or 30 fresh islets (handpicked islets of relatively uniform large size and round shape, which are about 300 μm in diameter), both containing similar numbers of cells, were used. The organoids or islets were first washed with Krebs buffer for 3 times, then pre-incubated in low glucose (2 mM) for 2 hr to remove residual insulin. Subsequently, the organoids were washed for 2 times in Krebs buffer followed by incubation in low-glucose for 30 min, and the supernatant samples were collected. Subsequently, the organoids were washed 2 times in Krebs buffer followed by incubation in high glucose (20 mM) for 30 min, and the supernatant samples were collected. This process was repeated for two additional times. Insulin/C-peptide levels contained in the supernatant samples were analyzed using mouse Ultrasensitive Insulin/C-peptide ELISA kit following standard protocols.

    Transmission electron microscope (TEM)

    To prepare samples for TEM, organoids or islets were fixed in 1% glutaraldehyde in 0.1 M sodium cacodylate buffer (pH 7.4) at 4°C overnight and post-fixed in 2% aqueous osmium tetraoxide at 4°C for 1.5 hr, then dehydrated in gradual ethanol (30% to 100%) and propylene oxide, embedded in Epon812 and cured for 48 hr at 60°C. 50 nm ultrathin sections were collected onto 200 mesh copper grids, stained with uranyl acetate (10 min) and lead citrate (5 min) before examination by transmission electron microscopy.
    For immunogold staining, organoids or islets cells were fixed in 4% PFA for 2-3 hr and treated with 0.5% Triton X-100 for 40 min before sample preparation.
    Images were captured with FEI Tecnai G2 Spirit Transmission electron microscope.

    Ca2+ imaging

    Organoids were harvested after dispase dissociation and plated in 35 mm glass bottom dish without Matrigel for 24 hr. The cell clusters were washed with Krebs buffer for 2 times and incubated with Fluo4-AM for 30 min. Afterward, Fluo4-AM was washed out with Krebs buffer, and the cell clusters were incubated in 37°C for an additional 15 min without the dye. The cell clusters were then staged on Nikon AI inverted confocal microscope for the acquisition of high-resolution time series images. Time series images were acquired every 10 s. Progression of glucose challenges and time of stimulation during imaging was as follows: 5 min in low glucose (2 mM), Krebs wash, 5 min in high glucose (20 mM), Krebs wash. This sequential treatment was repeated for two more times, and then a final incubation for 5 min with 2 mM glucose containing 30 mM KCl was performed. For the measurement of the average fluorescence intensity, images were quantified by Imaris software (v8.2.0).

    Karyotyping

    Early (2nd) and late (15th) passaged organoids were harvested at day 5-7 in culture, a phase with robust proliferation, followed by incubation for 20 hr with 1 μg ml-1 nocodazole. Then, organoid cells were dissociated into single cells and processed using standard karyotyping protocols. In detail, cells were incubated in 0.075 M KCl for 20 min and fixed with Carnoy’s Fixative (3:1 ratio of methanol: glacial acetic acid) for 25 min, for 3 times in total. Then, cells were dropped onto slides and air-dried, followed by treatment with 0.05% trypsin for 1 min and staining with DAPI for 2 min before mounting.

    Transplantation in diabetic mice

    Nude recipients were induced diabetes with a single intraperitoneal injection of streptozotocin (STZ, 160 mg per kg in pH 4.5 citrate buffer) 7 days prior to engraft. Nonfasted blood glucose was measured from tail vein samples with a OneTouch glucometer to select the diabetic recipients with blood glucose level increased to about 20 mM. 300 mouse islets or 1,000 organoids (7th or 15th passage, at day 7 culture) were engrafted underneath the kidney capsule of recipient mice. Nonfasted blood glucose and body weight were routinely measured after engrafting for 1 month and 4 months. Glucose tolerance tests (GTTs) were performed following standard protocol. In brief, mice were fasted overnight and received an intraperitoneal injection of 2 g per kg body weight glucose, blood glucose levels were measured at 0, 15, 30, 60, 90, and 120 min after glucose challenges. Serum samples before and after glucose injection were also collected and measured by ELISA. Recipients were sacrificed 1 month or 4 months after engrafting, and the grafted tissues were harvested for section and endocrine markers staining or digested into single cells for 10X sc RNA-sequencing. The recipients’ pancreatic islets were also harvested and stained with insulin antibody, ensuring the enduring effect of β cell ablation by STZ, and the glycemic amelioration was not affected by any potential islet regeneration in the pancreas. At 4 months after engrafting, nephrectomy was also performed to examine the impact of removing the grafted organoids or islets to the glycemic amelioration.

    RNA isolation and qPCR

    RNA isolation of primary cells or organoids were performed following the manufacturer’s instructions. Samples were lysed in RNAiso plus. Extracted RNA was reverse transcribed into cDNA using the Primerscript RT master kit. qPCR samples were prepared with SYBR Green Mixture and detected using Applied Biosystems Stepone Plus machine. All the qPCR primers were listed in Table S1.
