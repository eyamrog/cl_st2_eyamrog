P/AM 1

Effect of familial risk for breast cancer on workday-non-workday differences in the cortisol awakening response

LC Abernathy, GD James

Department of Anthropology, Binghamton University, Binghamton, NY

Studies have shown that the cortisol awakening response (CAR) on a workday is greater than that on a non-workday, but it is unknown as to whether this difference is affected by familial risk for breast cancer (FR) among women. The purpose of the present study was to compare the CAR as assed by salivary cortisol samples collected on a work and non-workday between healthy FR+ (n = 85, age = 33.07.4) and FH (n = 134, age = 34.27.8) premenopausal women. The women all worked a day shift and were studied in their luteal phase. Saliva samples were collected upon awakening and approximately 30 minutes afterward. Cortisol concentrations were determined using an enzyme immunoassay (Salimetrics, State College, PA, USA). CAR was calculated as the trapezoidal area under the curve. Both individual samples and the CAR were evaluated using repeated measures ANCOVA with FR included as a fixed factor. As prior studies have found that a variety of demographic characteristics are related to the CAR, they were included as covariates. The results show that there was no differences by FR in the CAR overall or by day. However, consistent with earlier studies, the CAR was higher on a workday than non-workday (P = .025), as was the cortisol level 30 minutes after awakening (P = .001). In addition, education level, ethnicity, and marital status were significant predictors of the CAR (P<.05). Awakening levels were not different. These results confirm reported CAR findings and suggest that although earlier studies show that FR impacts cortisol levels during the day, the CAR is unaffected.

PLENARY

What is normal bone health?

SC Agarwal

Department of Anthropology, University of California Berkeley, Berkeley, CA

A bioarcheological perspective on meaningful measures and interpretations of bone strength, loss, and aging Bioarcheology (the study of archaeological human remains together with contextual and documentary evidence) offers a unique approach to examine variation in skeletal morphology related to influences such as activity, disease, and nutrition. As a dynamic tissue that is forged by biocultural factors over the entire life course, the human skeleton provides a record of individual and community history. Various aspects of adult bone health, particularly bone maintenance and fragility of bone tissue, and the associated skeletal disease, osteoporosis have been examined in numerous past populations. The focus of bone loss in the past has been on females as a weaker sex and interpretation has traditionally been on the signature of normative aging and diminished reproductive capabilities and fragility, contrasted against the male signature of bone strength. However, empirical research on bone maintenance and aging in the Medieval archaeological record, together with comparative historical data, will be presented that show that patterns of bone loss do not constitute predictable consequences of aging or biological sex. Instead, the critical examination of bioarcheological data highlights the complex and changing processes that craft the human body over the life course, and calls for us to question the ideal or normal range of bone quantity and quality in the human skeleton, and to critically reflect on what measures are actually biologically and/or socially meaningful. The a priori expectation of normative sex-related bone loss/fragility in bioanthropological studies illustrates the wider gender-ideological bias that continues in research design and data analysis in the field.

P/AM 38

Using Doppler ultrasound to measure total cerebral blood flow as a proxy for global brain energy expenditure

JE Aronoff,1 CW Kuzawa1,2

1Department of Anthropology, Northwestern University, Evanston, IL; 2Institute for Policy Research, Northwestern University, Evanston, IL

The brain consumes a large fraction of the body's energy budget, and developmental changes in brain energy needs drive trade-offs with functions like body growth. Although these trade-offs are an important influence on patterns of energy allocation, investigating the brains changing energy needs across development, and in relation to nutritional and other environmental factors, is limited by the costs and logistical challenges of current methods. Positron emission tomography (PET) and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) provide good measures of glucose uptake and cerebral blood flow (CBF) but are costly and require access to sophisticated imaging facilities. Here we explore Doppler ultrasound as a non-invasive, portable and field-friendly means of quantifying CBFa strong correlate of cerebral glucose uptakeand compare CBF values in adults and in children who are near the age of the brain energetics peak. CBF was calculated as the sum of arterial blood flows through the left and right internal carotid arteries (ICA) and vertebral arteries (VA). In 10 Chicago-area adults, mean ICA values were 259.1 (SD 77.8), while mean VAs were 48.2 (SD 31.0) mL/min. Mean total blood flow was 614.6 (SD 151.8) mL/min. Using these data, and comparable values obtained in 4-7-year-old children, we will test the hypothesis that childhood CBF will be roughly double the adult mean. We conclude that Doppler ultrasound holds promise as a field-friendly method for measuring cerebral metabolism in studies of human development and life history

A missense variant in CREBRF, rs373863828, is associated with fat-free mass, not fat mass in Samoan infants

KJ Arslanian,1 UT Fidow,2 T Atanoa,3 T Naseri,4 RL Duckham, 5,6 ST McGarvey,7 S Oyama,1,8 CC Choy,9 EE Kershaw,10 NL Hawley11

1Department of Anthropology, Yale University, New Haven, CT; 2Obstetrics & Gynecology, Tupua Tamasese Meaole Hospital, Samoa National Health Services, Apia, Samoa; 3Community Studies Program, University of California-Santa Cruz, Santa Cruz, CA; 4Ministry of Health, Government of Samoa, Apia, Samoa; 5Deakin University, Institute for Physical Activity and Nutrition, Geelong, Australia; 6Australian Institute for Musculoskeletal Sciences (AIMSS), St. Albans, Australia; 7Departments of Epidemiology and Anthropology, International Health Institute, Brown University School of Public Health, Providence, RI; 8Yale School of Medicine, Yale University, New Haven, CT; 9Department of Epidemiology, School of Public Health, Brown University, Providence, RI; 10Division of Endocrinology, Department of Medicine, University of Pittsburgh, Pittsburgh, PA; 11Department of Chronic Disease Epidemiology, Yale School of Public Health, New Haven, CT

Infancy is a critical period that may shape risk for obesity later in life. In Samoa, where 80% of the adult population are living with obesity, studying the infant period may inform etiology and prevention efforts. Ours is the first study to examine infant body composition and the CREBRF gene variant, rs373863828, which has the largest known effect on adult Body Mass Index (BMI). We genotyped n = 117 Samoan infants and measured body composition using Dual Energy X-Ray Absorptiometry (DXA) in a prospective longitudinal study from birth to 4 months of age. We found the CREBRF phenotype in infants appeared to be different to that seen in adults: there was no difference in BMI or fat mass between infants with and without the variant. After controlling for relevant covariates, infants with the variant did, however, have greater bone mass (4.1 0.2g, P =0.002) and lean mass (292.4 10.2g, P =0.013) at four months, and faster lean mass growth from birth to four months (176.8 5.2g, P =0.018) than infants without any copies of the variant. Our results suggest the adult BMI-increasing' phenotype emerges sometime after four months. Additional research should validate our findings and in particular investigate lean and bone mass accretion as potential pathways to explain the seeming paradoxical protective effect of the CREBRF variant against diabetes in adults, which currently has no mechanistic justification. With forthcoming data from this cohort at 18-24 months we will model infant growth and examine effects of CREBRF in greater detail.

PODIUM B

Infant appetites are predicted by milk feeding and circulating leptin levels

KJ Barrett

Department of Anthropology, University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, Chapel Hill, NC

The importance of early growth in predicting later health outcomes is becoming increasingly apparent. Early growth is influenced in part by infant feeding, and parents feeding decisions are often made in response to infants behaviors. Infant appetite behaviors represent an important domain for research, but the origins of appetite remain unclear. Appetite may be associated with infant milk feeding (e.g., giving infants breastmilk and/or formula) and influenced by hormones such as leptin, which is produced in adipose tissue and promotes satiety in adults. This project uses longitudinal data from 47 infants in North Carolina to answer: 1) are infant milk feeding and leptin associated with infant appetites, and 2) does leptin mediate associations between infant milk feeding and appetites? Infant milk feeding, appetite measures, and dried blood spots were collected at three time points. Dried blood spots were analyzed for circulating leptin. Multilevel regression tested associations between infant feeding, leptin, and appetites. Circulating leptin was not associated with timing of energy intake. Leptin did not mediate but did attenuate associations between breastfeeding intensity and lower satiety responsiveness. Leptin also attenuated positive associations between later initiation of solid foods and enjoyment of food ratings. Higher breastfeeding intensity was associated with lower food fussiness, and leptin was independently associated with lower enjoyment of food, food fussiness, and slow eating. These findings suggest that infant milk feeding is an important predictor of infant appetite behaviors. Furthermore, leptin may be implicated in the long-term rather than acute regulation of energy consumption in infants. Genomic, physiological and social contributions to the survival of post-reproductive ethnically Tibetan women residing at high altitudes in Nepal

CM Beall,1 B Basnyat,2 SR Craig,3 A Di Rienzo,4 EA Quinn,5 D Witonsky4

1Anthropology Department, Case Western Reserve University, Cleveland OH; 2Oxford University Clinical Research Unit, Patan Hospital, Kathmandu NP; 3Department of Anthropology, Dartmouth College, Hanover, NH; 4Department of Human Genetics, University of Chicago, Chicago, IL; 5Anthropology Department, Washington University, St. Louis, MO

Heritable traits provide the raw materials for natural selection. Identifying relationships among human traits, genotypes, reproduction, and survival requires considering multiple sources of variation. 1,006 post-reproductive Tibetan women residing above 3000m in Nepal provided this information in 2012. Two loci in the oxygen homeostasis pathway showed the largest estimated allele frequency changes since divergence from an ancestral Han population. EGLN1 encodes a cellular oxygen sensing protein. EPAS1 encodes a transcription factor that induces genes required for the response to hypoxia. EPAS1 associated with unelevated hemoglobin concentration, which associated with greater reproductive success after controlling for sociocultural influences. We report on a seven-year follow-up of 553 of those women residing above 3500m in Upper Mustang, Nepal. Fifty-six had died, 45 had moved away; 428 participated in 2019. Surviving women averaged 53 +10 (SD) years at baseline compared with 64 +10 (SD) years among non-survivors. Survivors and non-survivors did not differ in hemoglobin concentration measured in 2012 nor in EGLN1 allele frequencies. Three EPAS1 SNP sites showed significant (P =102-103 corrected for multiple testing) allele frequency differences between survivors and non-survivors, although none associated strongly with hemoglobin concentration. Interpretation of these results is not straightforward; perhaps SNP sites affecting survival differ from those affecting hemoglobin concentrations. In contrast, married women and those with high Relative Wealth Rank (P <0.05 Chi-Squares) had higher reproductive success and higher chance of surviving. Studies of natural selection in people must incorporate sociocultural factors with comparatively large effects.

P/AM 22

Maternal investment and interbirth interval in Kilimanjaro, Tanzania

A Bell,1 K Lacy,1 M Gauck,1 M Duris,1 T Hopt,1 I Kiwelu,2 F Mowo,2 B Mmbaga,2 K Wander1

1Binghamton University (SUNY) Binghamton, New York, USA, 2Kilimanjaro Clinical Research Institute, Kilimanjaro, Tanzania

Interbirth interval (IBI) likely reflects, in part, maternal investment. Longer IBI may be the product of greater investment in each individual child, while shorter IBI may indicate a quantity approach in the face of lower resources, whether monetary or somatic.

Generally, greater maternal height and increased maternal age are associated with longer IBI. Because greater height is an indicator of greater overall maternal health, and older maternal age is an indicator of reproductive senescence and increased birth risks, each may result in a higher maternal investment strategy. Additionally, IBI are often longer after male children.

In this study, we evaluated the extent to which IBI reflects maternal investment among 106 women in the relatively prosperous population of Kilimanjaro, Tanzania (N = 77 with a preceding interbirth interval for analyses). We employed adjusted Akaike information criterion (AIC) to identify the best fit linear regression model of maternal and child characteristics associated with IBI. Selected variables include those predicted by theories of maternal investment as described above.

We observed longer IBI among older mothers (P =0.0003) and after a male index child (P =0.006), as predicted. Greater maternal height, on the other hand, was associated with a shorter IBI (P =0.032). This result may reflect better health and faster recovery from pregnancy among taller mothers, rather than parental investment strategy.

These results add to our knowledge of the circumstances that influence interbirth interval length, which plays an important role in both maternal and child health.

PODIUM C

Prevalence and predictors of dehydration across distinct water insecure contexts: The Water Insecurity, Stress, and Hydration (WISH) study among Tsimane' in lowland Bolivia and Daasanach in northern Kenya

H Bethancourt,1 Z Swanson,2 T Huanca,3 E Conde,3J Saunders,2 S Dhanasekar,4 S Young,5 R Nzunza,6 H Pontzer,2,7 A Rosinger1,8

1Department of Biobehavioral Health, Pennsylvania State University, University Park, PA; 2Department of Evolutionary Anthropology, Duke University, Durham, NC; 3Centro Boliviano de Investigacion y Desarrollo Socio Integral (CBIDSI), San Borja, Bolivia; 4Department of Anthropology, Emory University, Atlanta, GA; 5Department of Anthropology, Northwestern University, Evanston, IL; 6Kenya Medical Research Institute (KEMRI), Nairobi, Kenya; 7Global Health Institute, Duke University, Durham, NC; 8Department of Anthropology, Pennsylvania State University, University Park, PA

Understanding variation in the prevalence and predictors of dehydration across ecologically diverse contexts provides insight regarding thermoregulatory and behavioral adaptations to varying degrees of water insecurity. The Water Insecurity, Stress, and Hydration (WISH) study measured hydration (urine specific gravity, USG), household water insecurity, heat index, and sociodemographics following the wet season among 133 Daasanach households (N = 219 adults 18years, 102 children <18years) in a water-scarce region of northern Kenya and 262 Tsimane' households (N = 410 adults 18years, 230 children <18years) in a water-rich region of lowland Bolivia. Despite 93.2% of Daasanach households reporting water insecurity, prevalence of dehydration (USG>1.020) was 17.4% and 33.3% among adults and children, respectively. By contrast, only 1.9% of Tsimane' households reported water insecurity, yet the prevalence of dehydration was 57.6% and 42.2% among adults and children. Logistic regression models clustering standard errors on households and adjusting for age, sex, and time of day suggested that BMI (kg/m2) was positively associated with odds of dehydration among Daasanach adults (OR: 1.15; 95%CI: 1.04-1.27), whereas a 5-degree Fahrenheit increase in heat index was positively associated with odds of dehydration among Tsimane' adults (OR: 1.28; 95%CI: 1.10-1.51). Among children in both populations, time of day, but not BMI or heat index, was associated with increased odds of dehydration. Household-level water insecurity scores were not related to dehydration in either population. These findings suggest that distinct physiological, environmental, and lifestyle factors may interact to make some populations more vulnerable to dehydration regardless of water insecurity experiences.

The role of early life adversity and breast cancer outcomes

AM Boddy,1 S Rupp,2 HA Hanson,3,4 Z Yu,3,5 A Aktipis,6 KR Smith,3,5

1Department of Anthropology, University of California Santa Barbara, Santa Barbara, CA; 2Biodesign Institute, Arizona State University, Tempe, AZ; 3Population Science, Huntsman Cancer Institute, University of Utah, Salt Lake City, UT; 4Department of Surgery, University of Utah, Salt Lake City, UT; 5Department of Family and Consumer Studies, University of Utah, Salt Lake City, UT; 6Department of Psychology, Arizona State University, Tempe, AZ

Individuals that experience adverse childhood events have a higher lifetime risk of developing chronic diseases later in life, including cancer. Here we examine the effects of early life adversity between subtypes of estrogen receptor (ER) status breast cancer. While evolutionary mismatch hypothesis predicts ER positive breast cancer susceptibility, reproductive factors are not predictive of ER negative breast cancer. Using a life history framework, we hypothesize that early childhood adversity may calibrate the physiological systems towards accelerated reproduction at the expense of somatic maintenance, putting these women at a higher risk for ER- breast cancer. Population-wide data from the Utah Population Database (UPDB) and Utah Cancer Registry (UCR) are leveraged to test our hypothesis. Our cohort consists of 24,957 (n = 15,266 ER+; n = 4,281 ER) cases of women diagnosed with breast cancer spanning 20years (1990-2010) and 124,785 age-matched controls. Our data show patterns consistent with other studies where, where ER breast cancer cases show an earlier age of diagnoses (median: 58years ER; 62years ER+), poorer survival after diagnoses and an earlier age at death (median 70years ER; 79years ER+). In support of our main hypothesis, we find a significant positive relationship between early life adversity scores and ER diagnosis (P =0.003), but no relationship for overall breast cancer diagnosis (P =0.112). These data suggest that the effect of adverse childhood events varies across breast cancer subtypes, with measures related to a fast life history affecting a woman's risk for developing ER, but not ER+, breast cancer.

Exposure to water and brown adipose tissue activity in Micronesian college students: Preliminary results

DE Brown, D Anefal

University of Hawaii at Hilo, Hilo, Hawaii

Brown adipose tissue (BAT) is utilized for non-shivering thermogenesis as an adaptation to exposure to cold temperatures. Little is known about the effects of cold exposure during development or in recent times on amount of BAT activity. Accordingly, 15 students raised in Micronesia and attending the University of Hawaii at Hilo had their extremities exposed to a moderate cool water bath (17C) for five minutes with infrared thermography taken before and after the exposure. Change in maximum temperature in the supraclavicular area relative to temperature change in a control area above the sternum was used as an estimate of BAT activity. Participants were queried about their exposure to ocean water during childhood and in the past year, as water exposure is the primary means by which cold is experienced in Micronesia and Hawaii; an index of frequency of exposure was created. There was no significant relation between estimated BAT activity and water exposure index during development (water exposure in childhood: = 0.09, t = 0.3, ns), but there was a significant relation between estimated BAT activity and recent water exposure index in a backwards regression with water exposure, sex, BMI and age as predictor variables (recent water exposure: = 0.84, t = 3.0, P =0.01; sex: = 0.60, t = 2.2, P = 0.05). Past year cold exposure appears to be a more important determinant of BAT activity than exposure during childhood, but the relatively warm ocean water temperatures in Micronesia may also have affected these results. Changing Patterns of Breastfeeding and Weaning In Vanuatu: Initial Isotope Results

NM Burt,1 AB Roome,2 KN Dancause,3 Len Tarivonda,4 KM. Olszowy5

1Human Health and Evolutionary Medicine, Cleveland Museum of Natural History, Cleveland OH; 2Bassett Research Institute, Cooperstown, NY, Elisabeth Standard, Anthropology, Binghamton University, Binghamton, NY; 3Physical and Activity Sciences, University of Quebec, Montreal, QC; 4Vanuatu Ministry of Health, Port Vila, Vanuatu; 5Anthropology, New Mexico State University, Las Cruces, NM

Weaning and quality of weaning foods are important to health. WHO recommends exclusive breastfeeding for the first 6 months of life and breastfeeding until 2years or beyond. In Vanautu, a lower-middle income country in the South Pacific, mean breastfeeding duration is 18.7 months, with exclusive breastfeeding until only 4.3 months. This difference may seem small, but 2 months can make a huge difference to infant health. Early introduction of solid foods is associated with increased risk of micronutrient deficiency, obesity, and infection. Both adult diets and weaning foods in Vanuatu are changing as more processed foods are available. Stable isotope analysis (carbon and nitrogen) of hair was paired with traditional survey/interview data to understand the effect of processed food on diet and weaning behavior for mother-infant pairs in the Vanuatu communities of urban Efate and rural Aneityum islands. Hair samples were collected between June-August 2017. A total of 45 mother infant pairs were included in the study (Efate: Mothers n = 21, Children n = 22; Aneityum: Mothers and children n = 24). Initial results show that children at both sites have slightly enriched average 15N values (Efate 9.67; Aneityum 9.99) compared to mothers (Efate 8.59, Aneityum 8.54). All individuals show enriched 13C values with only a slight enrichment in samples from Efate. The individual 15N patterns in most children indicate a maintained level of mixed feeding (breastmilk and solid foods) regardless of place of residence, while some patterns may indicate weaning. Serial samples of mothers hair indicate a stable diet from month to month.

Excessive inflammation during pregnancy among low socioeconomic status women from Cebu City, the Philippines

MS Butler,1 SM Koning,1,2 SL Young,1,2 CW Kuzawa,1,2 J Borja,3 TW McDade,1,2

1Department of Anthropology, Northwestern University, Evanston, IL; 2Institute for Policy Research, Northwestern University, Evanston, IL; 3Office of Population Studies Foundation, University of San Carlos, Cebu, Philippines

Chronic inflammation has been implicated in cardiovascular and metabolic disease and is hypothesized to be a developmental mechanism linking early life conditions to adult health and disease. Inflammation dysregulation during pregnancy has additionally been associated with adverse birth outcomes in select, high-income countries. To clarify how these associations may shape health inequalities across generations, we investigated socioeconomic differences in inflammation during pregnancy using data from the Cebu Longitudinal Health Nutrition Survey, conducted in the Philippines (1983-present). Our sample included 344 women, with 457 pregnancies, between 2009 and 2014. We used linear regression to evaluate predictors of inflammation during the third trimester of pregnancy. Independent variables included level of education and income during pregnancy and we also controlled for confounding maternal and pregnancy characteristics. Final models will include C-reactive protein (CRP), cytokines IL6 and IL10, and the IL6:IL10 ratio, measured in dried blood samples. Education ( = 0.039, SE = 0.012, P <0.001) and income ( = 0.15, SE = 0.004, P <0.00) were inversely associated with IL6. Associations with education were attenuated in fully adjusted multivariate models ( = 0.021, SE = 0.014, P <0.155), but remained significant for income ( = 0.011, SE = 0.005, P <0.02). These findings highlight the potential importance of socioeconomic factors to the regulation of inflammation during pregnancy and suggest that inflammation may be an important mechanism for the intergenerational transmission of socioeconomic disparities in health.

Rural childhood health and life history in the southern United States: Lifestyle, immune function, and intestinal inflammation among children from Mississippi.

TJ Cepon-Robins,1 IC Recca,1, TE Gildner2

1Department of Anthropology, University of Colorado Colorado Springs, Colorado Springs, CO; 2Department of Anthropology, Dartmouth College, Hanover, NH

Children's health and development remain understudied in several parts of the United States, with many children in rural and low socioeconomic status regions experiencing inadequate nutrition, stunted growth, obesity, and exposure to infectious diseases. The present study is part of an ongoing research project exploring relationships between rural lifestyles, infectious and chronic disease burden, and growth/health among children in communities in the American south. Preliminary data collection in rural Mississippi in 2019 yielded finger-prick blood samples, family interviews, and anthropometric measurements for 32 children (ages 3-15years) from 17 families. Twenty children returned stool samples for intestinal inflammation analysis. 53% of families sampled reported incomes of <$10,000 per year, with 71% making <$20,000 per year. Preliminary analyses of stool samples for fecal calprotectin (FC), a measure of intestinal inflammation, documented clinically elevated levels (FC >50 ug/g) in 18 children (90%). The prevalence of elevated FC in this sample is higher than in previous studies of otherwise healthy children around the world, which range from 5% to 23%. Intestinal inflammation was not related to age. The local nurse practitioner reported a high prevalence of active Helicobacter pylori infection, an often-inflammatory bacterial infection of the digestive tract, among children in this community. Further analyses test relationships between FC, biomarkers of inflammation (Interleukin-6, C-reactive protein) and adaptive immune responses to macroparasitic and bacterial/viral infections (Immunoglobulin E and G), and lifestyle factors related to pathogen exposure in order to understand what may be contributing to elevated inflammation among children in this rural community.

P/AM 28

Endocrine disrupting chemicals and fertility in the United States (NHANES 2015-2016)

CB Chaney

Department of Anthropology, Yale University, New Haven, CT

The use of contextually relevant and locally adapted trauma scales may elucidate possible associations between traumatic stress and adolescent cortisol levels.

PEARL MEMORIAL SPEAKER

A new way forward for biocultural analysis of human population variation

AS Wiley

Indiana University, Bloomington, IN

Biological Normalcy

Biological normalcy is a new analytical framework for understanding human biological variation that considers the bi-directional relationships between the biology of populations and cultural norms. Specifically, it evaluates the relationships between normative social views of, and statistical norms for, human biological variation. Populations are characterized by statistical distributions - i.e. measures of central tendancy and variance for biological traits, which co-exist in societies with ideas about what constitutes normal human bodies, i.e. normative views about what bodies should be like. While statistical norms may carry no explicit evaluative weight, the question is how they are related to judgments about what is normal or abnormal. In a 1947 paper, Margaret Mead recognized their potential relationship: normalmay refer to the statistically usual in the culture usually without any recognition that this is culturally relative so that the statistically usual is identified with the basically human. Despite her observations over 70years ago, little has been done on this topic, yet such work promises new insights into the relationship between culture and biology, here described at the population level, rather than as individual genetic characteristics. Using examples, including my own work on human variation in the ability to drink milk, I outline the ways in which statistical norms may: influence individuals perceptions of what is normal (Meads basically human), lead to normative judgments about what human biology should be (ethno-biocentrism), which are reinforced by biases in discourse about human variation, and potentially feed back to mold the biological characteristics of a population.

Are universal or context-dependent measures of material wealth better predictors of biological well-being in low-and middle-income countries?

KL Woolard, DJ Hruschka

Arizona State University , School of Human Evolution and Social Change, Tempe, AZ

Access to economic resources is a well-established determinant of biological well-being. In the last decade, researchers have proposed a number of methods for comparing economic well-being in low-income countries, largely relying on assets (e.g. TVs) and access to services (e.g. sewer access). However, each method relies on different assumptions about the universality of asset weightings and there have been no systematic comparisons across these methods. We compared four emerging measures of wealth which differ in the degree to which they assume universal weightings of assets: the Absolute Wealth Estimate (AWE), the International Wealth Index (IWI), the Comparative Wealth Index (CWI), and the Standard of Living portion of the Multi-Dimensional Poverty Index (MPI). We used data from over 300 Demographic and Health surveys in 84 countries (n = 4,378,858 households, 282,439 women, 405,037 children) to examine 1) the correlations between the estimates from each of the three methods and 2) how well they predict three measures of biological well-beingadult BMI, child height-for-age, and likelihood of child mortality. We found that these four measures are all highly correlated (r = 0.80 to 0.90). Although all measures predicted a substantial portion of variance in the three measures of biological well-being, the measure based on universal asset weightingsthe international wealth indexaccounted for the largest portion of variation in all outcomes both between and within countries. We discuss the implications of these findings for choosing and interpreting these measures of wealth for different purposes.

1Department of Chronic Disease Epidemiology, Yale School of Public Health, New Haven, CT; 2Ministry of Health, Apia, Samoa, 3Lutia I Puava Ae Mapu I Fagalele, Apia, Samoa; 4Institute of Physical Activity and Nutrition, Deakin University, Melbourne, VIC, Australia; 5Australian Institute for Musculoskeletal Sciences, Melbourne, VIC, Australia; 6Department of Epidemiology, Brown University, Providence, RI

Anemia is a global public health problem with significant adverse health outcomes. In 2014, prevalence of anemia among children in the Pacific Islands ranged from 20% to 70%; in Samoan preschool age children, prevalence was estimated at 44.1%. There are, however, few longitudinal studies of anemia in young children in the Pacific so it remains unclear whether anemia is transient or persistent in this setting. Our study is the first longitudinal study to address this knowledge gap. Samoan children (n = 263) aged 2 to 5years were recruited in 2015 and followed up two-three years later (2017/2018) as part of the Ola Tuputupua'e Growing Up study in Samoa. More research, including examination of infectious disease burden, is needed to identify additional risk factors for chronic and transient anemia in this setting.

Time allocation differences by sex and age among egalitarian BaYaka foragers

C Younkle,1 MS Sarma,1 A Boyette,2 S Lew-Levy,3,4 V Miegakanda,5 LT Gettler1,6

1Department of Anthropology, University of Notre Dame, Notre Dame, IN; 2Max Planck Institute for Evolutionary Anthropology, Department of Human Behavior, Ecology and Culture, Leipzig, Germany; 3 Department of Psychology, Simon Fraser University, Burnaby, B.C., Canada; 4 Department of Archaeology and Heritage Studies, Aarhus University, Hjbjerg, Denmark; 5 Institut National de Sant Publique, Brazzaville, Republic of Congo; 6Eck Institute for Global Health, University of Notre Dame, Notre Dame, IN

All small-scale societies divide labor by sex, including in politically egalitarian foraging cultures. Yet, although social and productive roles change throughout the lifespan, relatively few time allocation studies in such settings have explored age-related trends in the division of labor of men and women, which may reflect shifting priorities and physical capacities with age. Here, through the use of single, ~3-hour morning focal follow data from BaYaka foragers in the Republic of Congo, we tested for sex differences and age-related patterning of time allocated to hunting, fishing/collecting, gardening, childcare, and domestic work. Across these domains, men (n = 17) spent the most time fishing/collecting and doing home-based work, whereas women (n = 20) spent the most time fishing/collecting and on childcare. Women and men did not significantly differ for time devoted to fishing/collecting, gardening, or domestic work. We found that women spent significantly more time doing childcare than did men, whose participation in childcare was relatively minimal. Men spent more time hunting than did women, who did no hunting during the follows. Older men spent more time on home-based tasks, but their hunting and childcare did not meaningfully vary with age, compared to younger men. Comparatively, older women did substantially less childcare than younger women. These data indicate that BaYaka men and women may differentially shift their allocations to work tasks with age and highlight the importance of considering how the sexual division of labor changes alongside adult male and female social roles across the life course.
